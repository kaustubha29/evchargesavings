Home charging guide

Where to plug in at home

90% of EV charging happens at home. Here's what each level means for your morning routine.

Level 1 · 120V outlet
4–5 miles/hr

Use your standard household outlet and the EVSE cord that came with your car. No installation needed — just plug in overnight.

Best for: low daily mileage (<40 mi/day), PHEVs, or as a backup
Setup cost: $0
Recommended
Level 2 · 240V / EVSE
20–35 miles/hr

A dedicated 240V circuit with a home EVSE (Level 2 charger). Most EVs fully charge in 6–10 hours overnight.

Best brands: Grizzl-E, JuiceBox, Emporia, ChargePoint Home Flex
Setup cost: $500–$1,500 installed · Rebates available in most states
DC Fast Charge · Public only
150–350 miles/hr

DCFC isn't available for home installation — it requires commercial electrical infrastructure. Use it on road trips or when you need a quick top-up.

Networks: Tesla Supercharger, Electrify America, EVgo, ChargePoint
Not available for home installation
Connector guide

NACS, CCS & CHAdeMO explained

The connector on your car determines which charging networks you can use — with or without an adapter.

NACS
NACS
North American Charging Standard

Originally developed by Tesla, NACS is now the industry standard adopted by most US automakers. Smaller and more ergonomic than CCS, with power delivery up to 500 kW in V4 Tesla stalls.

Cars using NACS

Tesla (all), Ford, GM (Chevy, Cadillac, Buick), Rivian, Honda Prologue, Polestar 2/3, Volvo, BMW (2025+), Ram, Jeep, Dodge

Compatible networks

Tesla Supercharger (native), Rivian Adventure, ChargePoint, EVgo · Electrify America needs CCS→NACS adapter

CCS
CCS
Combined Charging System

The previous industry standard, used by most non-Tesla EVs sold before 2024. CCS combines AC and DC charging in one connector. Most networks still support it and adapters are available.

Cars using CCS

Hyundai, Kia, Genesis, VW, Audi, Porsche, Mercedes, Nissan Ariya, Toyota, Subaru, Lucid, VinFast, Mini, Mazda, Jaguar

Compatible networks

Electrify America (native), ChargePoint, EVgo · Tesla Supercharger needs NACS→CCS adapter (~$249)

CHd
CHAdeMO
Legacy DC fast charge

A legacy fast-charging standard developed in Japan. The only mainstream US car still using CHAdeMO is the Nissan Leaf (2023 and earlier). No adapter to CCS or NACS exists — CHAdeMO is a dead end.

Cars using CHAdeMO

Nissan Leaf (2023 and earlier) · The 2024+ Ariya uses CCS

Compatible networks

Electrify America, EVgo (legacy CHAdeMO stalls) · Not compatible with NACS or CCS networks without adapters
